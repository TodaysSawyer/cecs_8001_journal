Maker Project

Covered: Skills 1, 2, 3

The Individual Maker Project was a key pressing matter and drew upon, to varying degrees, all three target skills. With a goal of building a “cybernetic” baby monitor, and in order of skills, I would need:

  1. Coding - writing a large library of bespoke Python, operating on a Raspberry Pi to support key objectives of environmental monitoring, cry detection and classification utilising a machine learning model, data logging and smart notifications, and a real-time video stream.
  2. Physical “making” - soldering sensors and mounting components.
  3. Project managing - setting a defined set of goals, working to an iterative model that aims to ensure a functional product at multiple intervals in line with hitting a strict demo-day deadline.

I found myself barrelling down ever more rabbit holes as I iterated from a simple BME280-based environmental sensor to a fully-featured prototype. To extract from CECS6001 Assessment 2B writings:

In the prototype phase, boundaries of the product will be strictly defined by the creator. In particular, the functions that sit outside these boundaries are:

  • Implementation of only a single degree of machine-learning enabled monitoring (audio).
  • No dedicated web interface (although camera feed was made available in a simple webform).
  • No dedicated applications for direct mobile interfacing.
  • No analysis of captured data or cross-referencing with health studies and outcomes.
  • No “feedback” of captured data or data analysis into the models and processes.

Further to this, the physical design of the prototype is limited as a bare Raspberry Pi with USB microphone, wired and bare environmental sensor, and ribbon-connected, unhoused, camera. The lack of housing, and inability to suitably mount the sensors without utilising twisted wire, is far from marketable or usable in an average household scenario.

Please refer to the following page for a schematic of the Maker Project, covering ultimate goal and prototype implementation, and feel free to review the committed code at https://gitlab.cecs.anu.edu.au/u7446851/cecs_8001_s1_maker(please ask me for access as required, as I’m not comfortable with making it public in the present state).

Prototype Behaviour

As depicted in the schematic, the prototype employs three forms of sensor; a camera for video, microphone for audio and BME680 for environmental monitoring. Whilst the video sensor is simply, in the prototype, passed to a locally hosted webpage for direct viewing without any other action derivation, the audio and environmental monitors are utilised to continuously log data:

  • Cry detection (based on self-trained TensorFlow machine learning model), and, if detected:
    • Cry classification (discomfort, hunger, tired, burping)
  • Environmental variables (takes 20 readings, averages all metrics, then reports):
    • Temperature
    • Humidity
    • Pressure
    • Gas (presence of compounds inferred from conductivity measure)
    • Internal Air Quality (IAQ) (as derived from above data, by Bosch’s proprietary formula)
    • IAQ accuracy (sensor calibrates on start-up, with accuracy slowly increasing to “3”)

For broad interoperability, logging was directed:

  • Locally for later collection and internal use.
